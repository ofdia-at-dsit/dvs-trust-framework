## 3. Terms and definitions

3.a. Please see the [glossary](../part-4/16-glossary-of-terms-and-definitions.md#section-16) for a full list of terms and definitions.

<a id="section-3_1"></a>

### 3.1. Digital identities and attributes

3.1.a. In the context of the trust framework and its supporting documents, a digital identity is a digital representation of a person. Unless otherwise stated, ‘person’ refers a natural person, in the sense of an individual human, rather than a legal person, like a company. A digital identity enables a person to prove who they are during interactions and transactions. It is also a way for organisations or users to check that other users are who they say they are. Digital identities can be used online or in person and may be created for single point-in-time use or be reusable.

3.1.b. Attributes are individual pieces of information that describe something about a person, like their age. In the context of the trust framework and its supporting documents, attributes are about individuals only, though may concern an individual’s relationship to an organisation. A combination of attributes can be used to create a digital identity and help people prove that they are who they say they are.

3.1.c. Attributes can also help prove a user is eligible or entitled to do something, such as buying age-restricted goods, or acting on behalf of an organisation. In these circumstances, it often is not necessary for the relying party to know the user’s full identity before they can complete an interaction, and attributes can be used on their own. In other situations, this proof could be added to an existing digital identity.

<a id="section-3_2"></a>

### 3.2. Levels of confidence

3.2.a. The trust framework enables identities to be checked to different ‘levels of confidence’. A level of confidence is a measure of how well an identity checking process protects against identity risks, based on the strength and the thoroughness of the checks performed on the claimed identity. Certified providers can prove identities to four levels of confidence:

- Low

- Medium

- High

- Very High

3.2.b. The trust framework itself does not set out how to reach a particular level of confidence. Instead, this is set out in a separately published guidance document called [‘How to check someone’s identity’](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/how-to-check-someones-identity-1-0) (also known as ‘Good Practice Guide 45’ or simply ‘GPG 45’).

3.2.c. The guidance outlines how to score different parts of the identity checking process and how to combine those scores into ‘identity profiles’. These identity profiles are then mapped onto the levels of confidence. The recognised identity profiles can be grouped by their corresponding level of confidence and include:

- Low level of confidence: L1A, L1B, L1C, L2A, L2B, L3A

- Medium level of confidence: M1A, M1B, M1C, M1D, M2A, M2B, M2C, M3A

- High level of confidence: H1A, H1B, H1C, H2A, H2B, H2C, H2D, H2E, H3A

- Very high level of confidence: V1A, V1B, V1C, V1D, V2A, V2B, V2C, V2D, V3A

<a id="section-3_3"></a>

### 3.3. Other terms used in this document

3.3.a. We use ‘provider’ to refer to an organisation that offers DVS under the trust framework. Where a provider has had their service or product certified against the trust framework, we refer to it as a ‘certified service’.

3.3.b. We use ‘the register’ to refer to the [register of digital identity and attribute services](https://www.digital-identity-services-register.service.gov.uk/), a public list of providers offering certified services established and maintained under [section 32](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/section/32) of the Act. Where a provider has a certified service listed in the register, we refer to them as a ‘registered provider’ and their service as a ‘registered service’.

3.3.c. We use ‘you’ and ‘your service’ in this document to direct a provider to the specific rules their organisation and service(s) must follow, and the recommendations they can follow, in order to be certified against the trust framework.

3.3.d. The word ‘must’ is used for any rules that a provider has to prove they have met in order for their service to be certified.

3.3.e. The words ‘can’, ‘may’ or ‘could’ are used to draw providers’ attention to optional standards and guidance they may wish to consider depending on their circumstances. However, these will not be requirements for certification.

3.3.f. We use ‘relying party’ to refer to an organisation that uses (or ‘consumes’) digital verification products or services. In the context of the trust framework, we primarily assume they are consuming digital identities and attributes from certified services, but relying parties may also provide attributes for certified services to assess.

3.3.g. We use ‘participant’ as a collective term to refer to all organisations that interact with the trust framework and take part in the wider digital identity and attributes market. This includes certified services and relying parties.

3.3.h. We use ‘supplementary code’ to refer to additional rules made under [section 29](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/section/29) of the Act that providers can certify against, alongside the trust framework, to demonstrate that they meet the requirements of a particular sector or use case.

3.3.i. We use ‘user’ to refer to a person who uses digital verification services to prove something about themself. This includes people with [delegated authority](../part-3/12-service-requirements.md#section-12_2) to act on behalf of somebody else, such as carers or parents.
